Manhattan Street Types

The borough's numbered street grid system is more than 200 years old and widely known for how easy it makes travelling on the island.

But what is the actual breakdown of streets vs. avenues? How does Manhattan's road network compare to the other boroughs?

By Holden Green



Manhattan's numbered grid system starts in the south, around Houston St. It's not as far south as the island stretches, but, in 1811 when the commissioner's plan was put into effect (the document that outlined how the grid would be laid out), the area south of Houston had already been settled for many years.

Instead, the plan showed a vision of Manhattan from 1st as its southern border to 155th street in the north that would be an orderly, simple system; prioritizing ease of navigation above all else.

A portion of the original 1811 Commissioner's Plan for Manhattan's street grid system.
(Courtesy of the Manuscripts and Archives Division, The New York Public Library)

The plan marks out the 155 numbered streets, which were to run east-west through Manhattan, and the 12 numbered avenues, which cover the length of the island in the north-south direction.

Now, more than 200 years later, Manhattan's population has skyrocketed. But, despite all the growth, the Commissioners' vision has endured and the borough remains largely adherent to the grid system they laid out.

But, what does the system actually look like now? How did the commissioners' plan all those years ago affect the breakdown of road types today?

Manhattan's road grid is dominated by streets and avenus; they make up over 75% of all roads in the borough. Streets alone stretch over 325 miles of Manhattan, striping it from Wall Street to its northern tip.

Streets are also more common in Manhattan than any other borough and they make up over 60% of the island's entire road network.

Clearly, the grid has taken hold in Manhattan more strongly than the other boroughs, and the straightforward streets/avenues pairing has remained a staple of Manhattan's road network.

Though the city has changed in ways the 1811 commissioners could have never imagined, their idea for an easily-navicable, straightforward Manhattan has prevailed.
